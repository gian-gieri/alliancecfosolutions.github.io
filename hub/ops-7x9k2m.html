Recurring Deliverables (Scalable Templates)

▸

A. 13-Week Cash Flow Forecast

The single most valuable deliverable for any small business. This is what separates a CFO from a bookkeeper.

Methodology

  1. Start with current cash balance — pull from bank accounts, not the books
  2. Map expected inflows week by week: recurring revenue, expected AR collections (by aging bucket), committed deposits, seasonal patterns
  3. Map expected outflows week by week: payroll (fixed cadence), rent/lease, loan payments, AP by due date, tax payments, recurring subscriptions, variable costs (COGS as % of revenue)
  4. Net weekly position = Starting cash + inflows − outflows
  5. Running balance — if any week dips below 2 weeks of operating expenses, flag it red
  6. Scenarios: Base case, best case (110% collections), worst case (75% collections + delayed AR)

Inputs Needed

  • Current bank balances (all accounts)
  • AR aging report (who owes what, when it's due)
  • AP aging report (what's owed, when it's due)
  • Payroll schedule and amounts
  • Recurring fixed costs (rent, insurance, loans, subscriptions)
  • Revenue pipeline / bookings / contracts
  • Known one-time expenses (equipment, taxes, bonuses)
AXEL Builds the first draft by pulling QBO data, bank feeds, AR/AP aging. Populates the 13-week grid. Flags any weeks where cash goes negative or below safety threshold. Takes ~20 min of AI time vs 3–4 hours manual.

GIAN Reviews assumptions (especially revenue timing and one-off expenses). Adds context Axel can't know: "That $50K receivable from Smith Co is going to be 2 weeks late — they always are." Validates, adjusts, approves. ~30 min.

B. Monthly Financial Review Memo

A 1–2 page narrative memo sent 2–3 days before the monthly review call. The client reads it, comes to the call prepared, and the call is strategic instead of educational.

Structure

  1. Executive Summary (3 sentences): What happened this month. Good, bad, and what to watch.
  2. Revenue: Actual vs budget vs prior month. Breakdown by service line or customer segment. Trend direction.
  3. Expenses: Total spend vs budget. Call out anything unusual (one-time costs, overages). Gross margin and net margin trends.
  4. Cash Position: Current cash, burn rate, runway. AR/AP health. Updated 13-week forecast summary.
  5. KPIs: 3–5 key metrics with trend arrows (↑↓→). Benchmarked against targets.
  6. Recommendations: 1–3 specific actions. Not generic advice — "Raise hourly rate on residential jobs from $X to $Y" or "Collect the $23K from ABC Corp this week."
  7. Questions for Discussion: 2–3 strategic questions to frame the monthly call.
AXEL Pulls all data from QBO, calculates variances, drafts the full memo including KPI section and initial recommendations. ~30 min AI time.

GIAN Reviews the draft, rewrites the recommendations section with strategic context, adds the "Questions for Discussion." Personalizes tone. ~20 min.

AI Agent Setup Service

▸

Positioning

This is not a separate business. It's a complementary service under Alliance CFO. You're already talking to business owners about their operations — when you see they're missing calls, losing leads, or drowning in admin, you offer this.

The pitch: "I'm your CFO. Part of my job is finding money you're leaving on the table. Right now, you're missing 30% of inbound calls. Let me fix that."

Target Market

  • Service businesses missing inbound calls (dental, law, HVAC, property management, contractors)
  • Businesses without a dedicated receptionist or with after-hours call volume
  • Anyone spending $3K+/mo on a front-desk person who primarily answers phones and books appointments

Pricing$2,000–$5,000 one-time setup + $500–$1,000/mo management & optimization
PlatformsVapi (primary — most flexible), Bland AI (simplest), Retell (best voice quality)

Sales Pitch Script

"Let me ask you something — when someone calls your office after hours or when your team's busy, what happens?" [They'll say: goes to voicemail / rings out / they call back later] "And what percentage of those people call back?" [They'll say: not many / maybe half / I don't know] "Industry data says about 80% of callers who hit voicemail never call back. They call the next business on Google. You're paying for marketing to get that phone to ring — and then losing the lead because nobody answered. Here's what I can set up for you: an AI receptionist that answers every call, 24/7, in a natural voice. It greets the caller, understands why they're calling, books appointments directly in your calendar, answers basic questions about your services and pricing, and texts you a summary of every call. It costs about 1/5th of a full-time receptionist and never calls in sick. I can have it live in about two weeks. Want me to show you a quick demo?"

Delivery Process

  1. Discovery (30 min call): Map their call flow — what types of calls come in, what questions get asked, how appointments are booked, what's the emergency protocol
  2. Script development (Axel): Build the conversation flows, FAQ responses, appointment booking logic, escalation rules
  3. Platform setup (1–2 days): Configure Vapi/Bland AI with the scripts, connect to their calendar (Calendly, Google Cal, or native), set up phone number forwarding
  4. Testing (2–3 days): Run 20+ test calls covering edge cases. Refine responses.
  5. Client review (30 min call): Walk them through the system, let them test it, get approval
  6. Go live: Forward their business number to the AI agent (or set up as after-hours overflow)
  7. Week 1 review: Analyze call logs, refine scripts, adjust based on real interactions
  8. Ongoing: Monthly call log review, script updates, performance metrics (calls handled, appointments booked, leads captured)

When to Hire a Technical Partner

At 10+ active setups per month, the configuration and testing time exceeds what Gian + Axel can handle. At that point:

  • Hire a part-time VA or contractor who specializes in Vapi/voice AI configuration
  • Cost: $2–3K/mo for a part-time specialist
  • Gian stays in the sales and relationship role; partner handles implementation
  • This should be self-funding by that volume (10 × $3K setup = $30K in setup fees alone)
Window: This opportunity has a 12–18 month window before it commoditizes. First movers who build client relationships now will retain them even as prices drop. Move fast.

SEO Audit Lead Gen Machine

▸

How It Works

Google Maps Scrape → Automated Website Audit → Branded PDF Report → Cold Email with Free Audit → Book Call → Close Remediation Package

Step-by-Step

  1. Scrape Google Maps — Target: service businesses in specific cities/niches (dentists in Denver, HVAC in Phoenix, law firms in Dallas). Pull business name, website, phone, email.

  3. Generate Branded PDF Report — Alliance CFO branded. Shows scores, issues found, estimated revenue impact. Professional, specific, useful — not a generic template.

  6. Book Call — 15-min call to walk through the report and pitch remediation.
  7. Close Remediation Package — Fix the issues found in the audit.

Discovery Call Framework

▸

5-Phase Structure (20–45 min)

This works for any prospect — CFO, AI agent, or advisory. Adapt the specifics, keep the structure.

Phase 1: Rapport (2–3 min)

"Hey [Name], thanks for making time. Quick background — I'm a CPA and business operator here in Colorado. I own businesses and work as a fractional CFO for owners like you. But before I talk about me — tell me what's going on."

Goal: Be human. Don't pitch. Let them talk first. Match their energy — if they're casual, be casual. If they're formal, meet them there.

Phase 2: Understand (10–15 min)

Ask 4–5 of these. Listen more than you talk.

  • "What's keeping you up at night financially?" — Opens the emotional door
  • "Walk me through how you currently see your numbers." — Reveals sophistication level
  • "When you make a big decision — hiring, expanding, taking on debt — how do you decide?" — Gut vs data
  • "What's your revenue and roughly how many employees?" — Qualifies size
  • "If you had a CFO sitting next to you right now, what's the first thing you'd ask?" — They tell you the scope
  • "What does the next 12 months look like for you?" — Growth, stabilization, sale?

Phase 3: Probe (5–10 min)

Go deeper on the pain they revealed. This is where you earn trust.

  • "You mentioned [X]. How long has that been a problem?"
  • "What's that costing you — not just in dollars, but in time, stress, missed opportunities?"
  • "Have you tried to solve this before? What happened?"
  • "If we could fix [X] in the next 90 days, what would that mean for you?"

Phase 4: Position (5–7 min)

"Based on what you're telling me, here's what I think is going on: [reflect their pain back clearly]. Here's how I'd typically approach that. Month one: I'd get my arms around your books and cash flow. You'd have a clear picture of where you actually stand. Month two: You'd have a dashboard and forecast you can actually use — no more guessing. From there, we work on the strategic stuff — pricing, margins, growth planning, the decisions that actually move the needle."

Mention the CPA credential naturally. Don't brag — let it land: "I'm a CPA, so I can also handle the tax planning side, which most fractional CFOs can't."

Phase 5: Close (2–3 min)

"I think there's a real fit here. Here's what I'd suggest — I'll put together a simple proposal with scope and pricing, and send it over by [specific day]. Take a look, ask questions, no pressure. Sound good?"

Always name a specific day for the proposal. "This week" is weak. "Thursday by noon" is accountable.

Objection Handling

ObjectionResponse
"That's more than I expected""I get that. Let me ask — what's the cost of continuing to make decisions without clear financial data? Most of my clients find one insight in the first month that pays for the entire engagement. If we find $5K in margin improvement on a $2,500 retainer, that's a 2x return in month one."
"I need to think about it""Totally fair. What specifically do you want to think through? I'd rather address any concerns now than have them linger." [If they push back: "No problem. I'll follow up [specific day]. What's the best way to reach you?"]
"The timing isn't right""When would be? I ask because most owners tell me the timing isn't right — right up until cash flow surprises them. What would need to change for the timing to feel right?"

"Can I just pay hourly?""I work on retainer because it aligns incentives — you get full access and consistent deliverables, I commit dedicated time to your business every month. Hourly creates a disincentive for you to call me. I want you to call me."

Client Transition Plan (CAS & Ascend)

▸

Current Situation

Colorado Asset Solutions (CAS) and Ascend Property Management are low-paying bookkeeping clients. They eat time, ping throughout the day, and represent the wrong type of work at the wrong rates. They need to go — but strategically.

TRIGGER: Do NOT transition until you have 2 advisory clients at $2,500+/mo secured. Never cut revenue before the replacement is in hand. Smart sequencing, not avoidance.

Three Options for Each Client

Option A: Raise Rates (Preferred First Step)

If the client is willing to pay advisory rates, keep them — but change the scope.

"Hey [Name], I want to talk about how we're working together. Over the past few months, I've been handling a lot of day-to-day bookkeeping tasks — transaction coding, reconciliations, data entry. That's important work, but it's not where I add the most value for you. What I'm really good at — and what I think would help your business the most — is the strategic layer: cash flow forecasting, financial analysis, helping you make better decisions with your numbers. Here's what I'd like to propose: I transition the bookkeeping work to a dedicated bookkeeper I trust, and I shift my role to a fractional CFO engagement. I'd oversee the bookkeeper's work, do the monthly financial reviews, build you a real forecast, and be your strategic finance partner. The bookkeeping would cost you about [$500-800/mo with a bookkeeper]. The CFO engagement would be [$2,500/mo]. Combined, it's more than you're paying now — but the value is in a completely different league. What do you think?"

Option B: Transition to a Bookkeeper

If they can't afford advisory rates, hand them off to a bookkeeper you trust.

"Hey [Name], I've been doing some thinking about how to best serve you. Honestly, what you need right now is consistent, reliable bookkeeping — and that's not my highest and best use for you. I'm a CPA focused on financial strategy, and I want to make sure you're getting the right service at the right price. I'd like to introduce you to [Bookkeeper Name], who specializes in exactly the type of work you need. They're reliable, detail-oriented, and I'll work with them during the transition to make sure nothing falls through the cracks. I want this to be a smooth handoff — I'll stay involved for 30 days to ensure the transition goes well. And if you ever need strategic financial help down the road, I'm here."

Option C: Release the Client

If they're a net negative (too demanding, too cheap, bad fit), let them go.

"Hey [Name], I've been evaluating my practice and I've made the decision to focus exclusively on fractional CFO advisory work going forward. That means I won't be able to continue providing bookkeeping services after [date — give 30 days notice]. I want to make sure you're taken care of — I can recommend a couple of bookkeepers who'd be a great fit. I'll also make sure all your files and access are organized for whoever takes over. Thank you for trusting me with your finances. I genuinely wish you the best."
